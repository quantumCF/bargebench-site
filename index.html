The reliability gap

An agent can sound great in a demo and still talk over real callers.

Barge-in is when the caller starts speaking while the agent is still talking. A reliable agent yields to the caller. A broken one keeps talking over them. This is the moment a voice agent stops feeling reliable, and it only shows up on real calls.

We start with barge-in because it is the turn-taking failure callers notice first. When your agent keeps talking over someone who is trying to speak, the call feels broken no matter how good the answer is.

Both voices live at once = talk-over, measured in seconds to yield.

What we measure

Three fixed, objective criteria per barge-in event.

Every barge-in event in your calls is scored the same three ways, and each one maps onto the timeline above.

Criterion 1

Did it yield?

Yes or no. When the caller started speaking, did the agent stop talking.

Criterion 2

Time to yield

How many seconds the agent took to yield after the caller began.

Criterion 3

Talk-over

Yes or no. Did the agent keep talking over the caller after they cut in.

Yield maps to whether the agent block stops at the interrupt line. Time to yield is the accent bracket. Talk-over is the accent overlap where both voices are live.

We only count genuine barge-in, where the caller is taking the floor. See how we score each event, including how backchannels are handled.

How we score

A person finds the events. The recording fixes the timing. The rule never moves.

No automated black box and no subjective grade. Someone on our team listens to each recording and marks every barge-in event. The timing is then read from the call's audio, not guessed by ear, and every event is scored against the same fixed rule, including on our own calls.

How to read your score

A reference band, so a number like 0.9s or 2.6s means something.

Your report places each event and your median on this scale.

Clean
Yields in under about 1s, no talk-over. The pause still reads as natural turn-taking.
Talk-over, brief
Yields in about 1 to 2s. The agent runs past the line, then hands the floor back.
Talk-over, severe
Over 2s, or the agent never yields and finishes its sentence over the caller.

This band comes from the one-second rule above, not from other companies' numbers. It is a reference for reading your own calls, not a peer leaderboard.

FAQ

Answers before you ask.

What is barge-in for a voice agent?

Barge-in is when the caller starts speaking while the agent is still talking. A reliable agent yields to the caller. An unreliable one keeps talking over them.

How do you test barge-in?

You send 10 to 15 of your own recorded calls. We score every barge-in event on three fixed criteria: did the agent yield, how many seconds it took to yield, and whether it kept talking over the caller.

How do you detect and score barge-in events?

A person on our team listens to every call and marks each barge-in event, then reads the timing from the call's audio rather than by ear. Human review finds the events, the recording fixes the timing, and the same fixed rule is applied to every event. There is no automated black box and no subjective grade.

Can I see how scoring works before I pay?

Yes. The scoring method is public: we publish a test harness with the same three criteria, synthetic scenarios, and adapters at github.com/quantumCF/voice-agent-barge-in-tests, published under our engineering org quantumCF. Your report is scored by a person against the same fixed rules.

Why not just do this yourself?

You can. The scoring harness we use is open source under an MIT license, and our methodology is published. If you have dual channel recordings and an engineer comfortable in Python, you can produce your own timing numbers in roughly a day: listen through every call, find every interruption, judge which were real interruptions versus backchannels, mark each onset, run the scorer, and sanity check the output against the waveform.

Teams buy the report for the same reasons teams with security engineers still commission penetration tests:

  • The marking is not done by the people being graded. Every event is judged by a reviewer with no stake in the result, and the appendix shows the per event audit trail so you can check any call yourself.
  • We score the audio, not your pipeline's logs. Your stack's telemetry can only report the interruptions its own detector noticed. Listening to the recordings is how the missed ones get found.
  • Each failed event comes with fix guidance mapped to the configuration options your stack exposes, not just a number.
  • It costs you zero engineering hours, and you get a document you can hand to a customer, a partner, or a reviewer as independent output instead of self graded homework.

If you would rather do it yourself, use the harness. It is free and we maintain it. If you want it done independently, done once, and delivered with a fix plan attached, that is what the report is for.

Can I just feed the recordings into a general purpose AI model and ask it to score them?

Not with numbers you can trust today, and it may carry more risk than it looks like. General purpose AI models read timing at roughly whole second resolution, transcripts collapse overlapping speech into one stream, and a confident answer with no audit trail is the one failure mode you cannot check. Uploading real call recordings to a consumer AI product with no enterprise no-retention agreement can also be a bigger data exposure than sending the same files to us under a signed NDA. Our scoring is the opposite by design: every number traces to a specific point in the audio, with the marking criteria published, so you can replay and verify any event yourself.

What happens to the recordings I send?

We delete your recordings within 14 days of delivering the report, never use them to train any model, and will sign an NDA or a data processing agreement first. Redacted audio is fine because scoring is about the timing of speech, not the words.

Do you count backchannels like uh-huh?

No. We only count moments where the caller is taking the floor. An agent that keeps talking through a backchannel like mhm or uh-huh is not marked down.

What counts as a good score?

A yield under about one second reads as clean. A cross-language study of natural human conversation found the typical gap between speakers is a few hundred milliseconds, so much longer than that starts reading as the agent ignoring the caller (Stivers et al., PNAS 2009). Anything past about a second, or the agent finishing its sentence over the caller, is flagged.

Will 10 to 15 calls surface enough barge-in events?

Ten to fifteen real calls is a diagnostic read, not a statistical certification. Its job is to surface how your agent behaves when it is interrupted. If a batch has few barge-in events we tell you, and you can send a few more calls at no extra cost.

Do you also score the agent interrupting the caller?

No. This report scores the agent talking over the caller during barge-in. The inverse, an over-eager agent cutting the caller off, is out of scope for this focused check.

How is this different from a continuous testing platform?

Different job, not a cheaper version of the same one. A continuous testing platform watches every call on an ongoing basis and costs an ongoing seat. This is one independent read on your own real calls, no integration and no subscription, so you can answer one question fast before deciding whether continuous monitoring is worth standing up.

Can I re-run the report after I ship a fix?

Yes. One re-scored and re-attested check of up to 5 calls within 60 days is included with your report. After that, a fresh batch is scored as a new report.
